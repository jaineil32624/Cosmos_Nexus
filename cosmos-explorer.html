Chapter I

Spacetime — The Fabric of Reality

Einstein's 1915 General Theory of Relativity revealed that space and time are not separate stages — they are woven into a single four-dimensional fabric called spacetime.

What Is Spacetime?

Every event in the universe has four coordinates: three in space (x, y, z) and one in time (t). Nothing can travel faster than light, and that speed limit is baked into the geometry of spacetime itself.

Massive objects warp this fabric, creating curvature that we experience as gravity. The more massive an object, the deeper the dent it creates.

Speed of light: 299,792,458 m/s 4 dimensions: (x, y, z, t) c² links mass & energy

Time Dilation

One of Relativity's strangest predictions: time flows at different rates depending on your speed and gravitational field depth.

Astronauts on the ISS age slightly differently than us. GPS satellites must correct for both effects every day, or navigation errors would accumulate by kilometers.

t′ = t / √(1 − v²/c²)

Lorentz time dilation — a moving clock ticks slower

Chapter II

Gravity — The Curve That Governs All

Gravity is not a force in the traditional sense. It is the geometry of curved spacetime directing the paths of masses — and even light.

Newton vs. Einstein

Newton described gravity as an invisible attractive force between masses: F = Gm₁m₂/r². This model worked brilliantly for 200 years. But it broke down near very massive objects.

Einstein replaced the concept with geometry: mass tells spacetime how to curve, and spacetime tells mass how to move. Light bends around massive objects — confirmed during the 1919 solar eclipse.

Gravitational Waves

When massive objects accelerate — like two colliding black holes — they send ripples through spacetime. These travel at the speed of light, stretching and compressing space.

First detected in 2015 by LIGO from a collision 1.3 billion light-years away. The signal stretched LIGO's 4-km arms by just 10⁻¹⁸ metres.

First detection: Sept 14, 2015 LIGO sensitivity: 10⁻¹⁸ m

Escape Velocity

Chapter III

Planetary Motion — Kepler & Beyond

The planets trace elliptical orbits around the Sun, governed by Kepler's three laws — discovered 66 years before Newton explained why they work.

Kepler's Three Laws

1. Elliptical Orbits — Planets travel in ellipses with the Sun at one focus, not perfect circles.

2. Equal Areas — A line from the Sun sweeps equal areas in equal times. Planets speed up near the Sun.

3. Harmonic Law — T² ∝ a³ — farther planets have much longer years.

The Solar System at Scale

Our solar system contains 8 planets, hundreds of moons, millions of asteroids, and billions of comets.

The Sun holds 99.86% of all mass. Its gravitational reach extends ~100 AU — where the solar wind finally yields to interstellar space.

1 AU = 149.6 million km Sunlight to Earth: 8.3 min

Why Don't Planets Fall Into the Sun?

Planets are perpetually falling toward the Sun — but they also have sideways velocity. In a vacuum with no friction, they keep moving sideways while falling, and the curve of their fall exactly matches the retreating Sun. This balanced free-fall is called an orbit.

Chapter IV

Stellar Life Cycles — Birth, Life & Death of Stars

Stars are cosmic furnaces that forge every element heavier than hydrogen. Their life story — from nebula to supernova — writes the periodic table.

Stellar Nucleosynthesis

At a star's core, temperatures exceed 15 million °C. Hydrogen fuses into helium, releasing the energy that keeps the star shining for billions of years. As hydrogen is exhausted: He → C → O → Ne → Si → Fe.

Iron is the endpoint. Fusing iron absorbs energy rather than releasing it — triggering the collapse that causes a supernova.

We Are Stardust

Every atom of carbon in your body, every oxygen you breathe, every iron atom in your blood — all forged in the cores of stars that lived and died before our solar system formed.

H → He (main sequence) He → C, O (red giant) Si → Fe (supergiant) Fe → heavy elements (supernova)
Chapter V

Black Holes — Where Spacetime Ends

A black hole is a region where gravity has curved so severely that not even light can escape. They are the densest objects that can exist.

Click anywhere in the visualization to send particles spiraling in.

Stellar

3–100 solar masses. Form when massive stars collapse. The most common type.

Intermediate

100–100,000 solar masses. Rare and poorly understood. Found in globular clusters.

Supermassive

Millions to billions of solar masses. Every large galaxy has one at its center.

The Event Horizon

The point of no return. For a non-rotating black hole: rs = 2GM/c². Earth compressed to a black hole: rs ≈ 9 mm.

Hawking Radiation

Stephen Hawking showed black holes slowly emit thermal radiation due to quantum effects at the event horizon. Over vast timescales they theoretically evaporate completely.

Cosmology — The Story of Everything

Cosmology asks the deepest questions: How did the universe begin? What is it made of? What is its fate?

Dark Matter & Dark Energy

Ordinary matter — everything we can see — makes up only 5% of the universe. Dark matter (27%) holds galaxies together. Dark energy (68%) is driving accelerating expansion.

■ Matter 5% ■ Dark Matter 27% ■ Dark Energy 68%

The Expanding Universe

Edwin Hubble discovered in 1929 that galaxies are moving away from us — the further they are, the faster they recede. In 1998 astronomers discovered this expansion is accelerating, driven by dark energy.

The Hubble constant H₀ ≈ 67–73 km/s/Mpc — for every megaparsec of distance, galaxies recede ~70 km/s faster.

Chapter VII

The Mathematics of the Cosmos

The universe speaks in mathematics. These equations are the grammar through which physicists read its deepest secrets.

Mass–Energy Equivalence
E = mc²

Mass and energy are interchangeable. A tiny amount of mass contains enormous energy — the principle behind nuclear fusion in stars.

Newton's Gravitation
F = Gm₁m₂/r²

Gravitational force falls off with the square of distance. Double the distance → quarter the force.

Hubble's Law
v = H₀ · d

A galaxy's recession speed is proportional to its distance. H₀ measures the expansion rate of the universe.

Schwarzschild Radius
rs = 2GM/c²

The radius at which an object becomes a black hole. Compress any mass inside this radius and its gravity becomes inescapable.

Kepler's Third Law
T² = a³

An orbit's period squared equals its semi-major axis cubed (in solar units). Farther planets have much longer years.

Planck–Einstein Relation
E = hf

The energy of a photon equals Planck's constant times its frequency. Higher frequency (bluer) light carries more energy.

Einstein's Field Equations

Gμν + Λgμν = (8πG/c⁴) Tμν

Einstein's master equation of General Relativity. The left side describes the curvature of spacetime. The right side describes mass and energy. Matter tells spacetime how to curve; curved spacetime tells matter how to move.
