Chapter I

Spacetime — The Fabric of Reality

Einstein's 1915 General Theory of Relativity revealed that space and time are not separate stages — they are woven into a single four-dimensional fabric called spacetime.

What Is Spacetime?

Every event in the universe has four coordinates: three in space (x, y, z) and one in time (t). Nothing can travel faster than light, and that speed limit is baked into the geometry of spacetime itself. Massive objects warp this fabric, creating curvature we experience as gravity.

Speed of light: 299,792,458 m/s 4 dimensions: (x, y, z, t) c² links mass & energy
Time Dilation

Time flows at different rates depending on speed and gravitational field depth. GPS satellites must correct for both effects daily — uncorrected, navigation errors would accumulate by kilometers.

t′ = t / √(1 − v²/c²)

Lorentz time dilation — a moving clock ticks slower

Chapter II

Gravity — The Curve That Governs All

Gravity is not a force in the traditional sense. It is the geometry of curved spacetime directing the paths of masses — and even light.

Newton vs. Einstein

Newton described gravity as F = Gm₁m₂/r². Einstein replaced the concept with geometry: mass tells spacetime how to curve, and curved spacetime tells mass how to move. Light bends around massive objects — confirmed during the 1919 solar eclipse.

Gravitational Waves

When massive objects accelerate — like two colliding black holes — they send ripples through spacetime at the speed of light. First detected Sept 14, 2015 by LIGO from a collision 1.3 billion light-years away. The signal stretched LIGO's 4-km arms by just 10⁻¹⁸ metres.

First detection: Sept 14, 2015 LIGO sensitivity: 10⁻¹⁸ m
Escape Velocity

To escape a planet's gravity without further thrust, you must reach its escape velocity. Earth's is 11.2 km/s. For a black hole — escape velocity exceeds the speed of light.

Chapter III

Planetary Motion — Kepler & Beyond

The planets trace elliptical orbits around the Sun, governed by Kepler's three laws — discovered 66 years before Newton explained why they work.

Kepler's Three Laws

1. Elliptical Orbits — Planets travel in ellipses with the Sun at one focus.

2. Equal Areas — A line from the Sun sweeps equal areas in equal times. Planets speed up near the Sun.

3. Harmonic Law — T² ∝ a³ — farther planets have much longer years.

The Solar System at Scale

Our solar system contains 8 planets, hundreds of moons, millions of asteroids, and billions of comets. The Sun holds 99.86% of all mass.

1 AU = 149.6 million km Sunlight to Earth: 8.3 min

Chapter IV

Stellar Life Cycles — Birth, Life & Death of Stars

Stars are cosmic furnaces that forge every element heavier than hydrogen. Their life story — from nebula to supernova — writes the periodic table.

Stellar Nucleosynthesis

At a star's core, temperatures exceed 15 million °C. Hydrogen fuses into helium. As hydrogen is exhausted: He → C → O → Ne → Si → Fe. Iron is the endpoint — fusing iron absorbs energy, triggering collapse.

We Are Stardust

Every carbon atom in your body, every oxygen you breathe, every iron atom in your blood — all forged in the cores of stars that lived and died before our solar system formed.

H → He (main sequence) He → C, O (red giant) Fe → heavy elements (supernova)
Chapter V

Black Holes — Where Spacetime Ends

A black hole is a region where gravity has curved so severely that not even light can escape. They are the densest objects that can exist.

Click anywhere in the visualization to send particles spiraling in.

Stellar

3–100 solar masses. Form when massive stars collapse. Most common type.

Intermediate

100–100,000 solar masses. Rare and poorly understood. Found in globular clusters.

Supermassive

Millions to billions of solar masses. Every large galaxy has one at its center.

The Event Horizon

The point of no return. rs = 2GM/c². Earth compressed: rs ≈ 9 mm.

Hawking Radiation

Black holes slowly emit thermal radiation due to quantum effects at the event horizon, and may eventually evaporate completely over vast timescales.

Ordinary matter makes up only 5% of the universe. Dark matter (27%) holds galaxies together. Dark energy (68%) drives accelerating expansion.

The Expanding Universe

Edwin Hubble discovered in 1929 that galaxies are moving away from us. In 1998, astronomers found this expansion is accelerating, driven by dark energy. The Hubble constant H₀ ≈ 67–73 km/s/Mpc.

Chapter VII

The Mathematics of the Cosmos

The universe speaks in mathematics. These equations are the grammar through which physicists read its deepest secrets.

Mass–Energy Equivalence
E = mc²

Mass and energy are interchangeable. A tiny amount of mass contains enormous energy — the principle behind fusion in stars.

Newton's Gravitation
F = Gm₁m₂/r²

Gravitational force falls off with the square of distance. Double the distance → quarter the force.

Hubble's Law
v = H₀ · d

A galaxy's recession speed is proportional to its distance. H₀ measures the expansion rate of the universe.

Schwarzschild Radius
rs = 2GM/c²

The radius at which an object becomes a black hole. Compress any mass inside this and escape becomes impossible.

Kepler's Third Law
T² = a³

An orbit's period squared equals its semi-major axis cubed (solar units). Farther planets have much longer years.

Planck–Einstein
E = hf

The energy of a photon equals Planck's constant times its frequency. Higher frequency (bluer) light carries more energy.

Einstein's Field Equations
Gμν + Λgμν = (8πG/c⁴) Tμν

The left side describes the curvature of spacetime. The right side describes mass and energy. Matter tells spacetime how to curve; curved spacetime tells matter how to move.
